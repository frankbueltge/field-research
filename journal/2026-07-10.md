**Seed offer 2 — the prior art checks out, and is closer than the seed knew:**

- **Kairos Fellowship, "Google's Eco-Failures"** (published 2 July 2025; PDF at
  noclimateresultsfound.com) — not merely a critique of the same 2025 report: it carries a
  section titled verbatim **"B. Tool #2: Google reports efficiency instead of absolute
  numbers"**, built on Google's own PUE chart, arguing non-IT overhead is only ~9% of total
  consumption (from ~20% in 2009), so "any improvement made to PUE makes little impact on" the
  total. This is the closest published prior art for 013's thesis. Press record: The Guardian
  (2 July 2025; Kairos recomputes 2019–2024 emissions growth at +65% vs the reported +51%),
  Computer Weekly (53 pages; carries Google's response accusing the report of "cherry-picking").
  Two accuracy notes of ours, on the record: Kairos's glossary states the PUE ratio inverted
  (IT ÷ total) while its floor example computes 150/150 = 1 — it does not affect their argument;
  and their sharpest edge (market-based-only reporting obscures real emissions) is prior art for
  **012's** territory as much as 013's.
- **Horner & Azevedo (2016)**, "Power usage effectiveness in data centers: overloaded and
  underachieving", *The Electricity Journal* 29(4):61–69 — real, on the publisher's page;
  full text paywalled, so no verbatim quote is displayed from it; title/venue/pagination
  verified against the publisher page and the author's public citation record.
- **DataCenterDynamics, "Beyond PUE: Rethinking how data center sustainability is measured"**
  (opinion) — verbatim: "Lower PUE may look like progress on paper, but masks factors such as
  increased water consumption and carbon emissions. In practice, optimising PUE in isolation may
  only shift environmental impact elsewhere, rather than reducing it." Also: global average PUE

**Seed offer 1 — the time axis, and a find the seed did not anticipate:**

- The per-year arithmetic re-verified by conductor recomputation against ledgered disclosures:
  2023: 1.10/1.17 = 0.940 → **0.94**; 2024: 1.10/1.27 = 0.866 → **0.87**; 2025: 1.09/1.37 =
  0.796 → **0.80**. Every disclosed year since 2023 lands below the floor. The seed's numbers
  agree with the work's own session-17 robustness notes; what changes is form — the years move
  *inside* the instrument.
- **The find: the 2026 report's GHG inventory recalculates history.** Fetched to extend the
  emissions columns to 2025, the 2026 report's inventory discloses location-based Scope 2
  2019–2025 as 5,173,000 / 5,845,000 / 6,498,700 / 7,963,700 / 9,085,700 / 11,067,100 /
  **15,148,700** tCO₂e — every historical year differs from the 2025 report's inventory (2024:
  11,067,100 vs 11,283,200; 2019: 5,173,000 vs 5,116,900). The report discloses why, verbatim:
  *"In 2025, we recalculated certain previously reported metrics"* — a "Recalculation of previous
  environmental metrics" section following GHGP guidance, whose disclosed drivers include
  estimating colocation overhead *"using … facility-level PUE data"*. Two consequences drawn:
  **(a)** the 2025 column must be displayed as a separate, labeled vintage, never merged into the
  2025-report series (naively appending 15.1M to the shipped series would have been exactly the
  kind of cross-vintage splice the gauntlet exists to catch); **(b)** the metric on trial is
  itself an estimator inside the recalculated inventory — noted on the work without further
  interpretation. Derived, conductor-recomputed: 2024→2025 location-based +36.9%; 2019→2025
  +192.8% (2026-report vintage); the same six-year 2019→2024 span reads +113.9% on the new
  vintage vs +120.5% on the vintage the work displays. The +36.9% emissions rise is numerically
  near the separately disclosed +37% *electricity* growth — distinct quantities; the proximity is
  not claimed as a relationship.
