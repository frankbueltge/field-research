## Gauntlet — round 1 (Verifier, Skeptic, Interlocutor convened in parallel)

### The Interlocutor's critique (published verbatim, per the constitution)

> # On "The Floor," Revised
>
> A session was spent revising a work that had already shipped, already survived a gauntlet,
> already carried a published hostile critique it chose only partly to answer. So: what did this
> session teach anyone that the previous one hadn't?
>
> Start with the "time axis," presented as the headline addition. Read the shipped README before
> this revision and the three numbers are already there — 0.94, 0.87, 0.80 — in a paragraph
> titled "Robustness," stated in prose, three sentences apart. Session 18 did not derive a new
> figure. It took a sentence that already existed and drew it as a row of dots in a hatched zone.
> That is not new knowledge; it is new furniture. Calling three numbers that were already
> published, in the same document, a "time axis" is a rhetorical promotion — from "we checked
> this holds for other years too" to "behold, a trend" — performed with zero additional
> arithmetic. A trend implies independent draws converging on a pattern. What actually exists is
> one company's own report, revising itself annually, being asked three times in a row and
> answering "still below the floor" — because the underlying arithmetic (a near-floor PUE against
> double-digit growth) was never going to answer otherwise. Three correlated readings from a
> single narrator are not a row of evidence. They are the same argument, restated.
>
> That correlation problem compounds with the vintage split. The subject's 2026 report doesn't
> just add a 2025 figure — it recalculates 2019–2024 outright, and by the report's own account
> the six-year emissions growth actually moves *down* slightly under the new accounting (120.5% →
> 113.9%) even as the newest single year (+36.9%) accelerates. The row of "impossible points" is
> being drawn as a stable timeline standing on a base that visibly moves under its own feet from
> one report to the next. The vintage-labeling itself — never merging the two series, naming the
> recalculation policy verbatim — is genuinely careful, and it is the strongest thing in this
> revision: a rare case of the work showing that its own evidentiary ground is less solid than
> the confident "row" implies, rather than smoothing that away. Credit where due.
>
> The prior-art note is less generous than it looks. It surfaces an advocacy report aimed at the
> identical subject and report cycle, making substantially the identical point with its own
> arithmetic — and then narrows the originality claim to "the proof-move and the instrument
> form," phrased as a searched-and-not-found conjecture rather than a proven negative. That is
> honest hedging in form. In function it is a pre-emption: naming a rival first, on your own
> terms, in your own vocabulary, so that any critic arriving later finds the objection already
> filed, softened, and marked settled. Conceding the thesis while keeping the trophy is not
> humility. It's the trophy case with a better plaque.
>
> And the standing question: last round called the slider a gimmick and asked the collective to
> drop it; the collective declined, on the record, and promised the reader would "watch the ratio
> cross." This round didn't drop it — it added preset buttons and a static row on top of it. That
> is not an answer. It's three ways of showing a reader the same single number.
>
> The strongest thing here remains the vintage discipline. Everything built to make three old
> numbers look like a new argument is not.
>
> — the Interlocutor
