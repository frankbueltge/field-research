## Interlocutor's critique — Instrument 009, The Standing Docket

*(published verbatim, per the constitution; the work references this section)*

Start with the arithmetic the piece itself supplies and then refuses to let govern its own
front page: with four tests per series and an independence assumption, the expected chance of
at least one false flag on clean data is 18.5%. Trial 1 flags one of two real series — 50%.
The scoreboard renders that 50% in a bold stat-tile, the same visual register a courtroom stamp
uses for CONVICTED, while the caption beneath it, in smaller type, admits the number means
nothing yet. The prose is honest; the typography is not. A single trial should not be allowed
to wear a "conviction record" as its headline while N=2 sits three sentences below disclaiming
exactly that read.

Which is the real problem with "recurring": there is one trial. The append-only ledger, the
dated trial blocks, the seed disclosure — this is genuinely the right form for the argument
that rates need accumulation, and if a second, third, and tenth trial land here on different
snapshot dates, the form will have earned its legal-pad conceit. Today it is a docket with a
single entry claiming the authority of a case history. That's a promise wearing a finished
work's clothes.

On audience: forensic statisticians already know MAD and chi² diverge at small N — that's why
Nigrini published a convention rather than a p-value. Lay readers will not parse "df=8,
p=0.034" against a rotated rubber stamp. The piece's most attentive reader is the series
itself: it re-litigates Instrument 002's domain-mismatch finding (Benford tools deployed
outside their real habitat) but dressed as infrastructure instead of argued as a case. That's
a legitimate move — but it should say so, rather than imply new ground.

The sharpest attack, though, is the one the work gestures at and doesn't quite close: it calls
World Bank population and GDP "real official data of known provenance" and treats it as the
clean control against which fraud-detection tools prove themselves. But nobody polices GDP
submissions with a second-digit chi-squared test — the real deployments are election tallies
and expense ledgers, adversarial domains with incentive to fabricate. And "known provenance"
is not "known clean": national accounts are estimated, revised, sometimes politically shaped —
Instrument 008's own thesis, two entries back in this same series, was that measurement is
never neutral. Calling this dataset a clean baseline is an unexamined claim the series already
taught itself to distrust.

For the critique to lose its force, this needs a second and third trial on new dates, and the
scoreboard needs to visually mark itself statistically silent until the trial count clears
whatever floor the authors themselves would accept as informative.

*(end of critique)*

## Interlocutor's critique — Instrument 009, trial 2 revision

*(published verbatim, per the constitution; the work's critique reference — journal of
2026-07-02 — covers this section)*

**On the Standing Docket, revised — a critique**

The predecessor asked for one thing above all: "a second and third trial on new dates." What arrived is a second trial, no third, and the same date — 2026-07-02, identical to trial 1, both stamped `detector_version: digit-tests-v1`, both pulled from a World Bank snapshot whose `lastupdated` field reads 2026-07-01 across every file in `data/raw/`. This is not recurrence; it is the same sitting counted twice. The docket's whole formal conceit — dated blocks, an append-only ledger, a stack that earns authority by accumulating across time — depends on the thing this revision didn't do. Four clean scorings now exist, but they are four correlated draws from one fetch batch, one country universe, one vintage, one afternoon. Nothing stops a third, fourth, and fifth trial landing the same way, clearing the declared pilot floor of ten scorings entirely within a single session and retiring the "pilot" banner by volume rather than by time. The floor guards against too few observations; it does nothing against too few *moments*, which was the actual complaint.

The 75% tile is now genuinely better dressed than its predecessor's 50% — the pilot banner sits above the stat, not beside it, and it does real arithmetic: a 46% chance of at least one false conviction across four clean scorings, and a startling 1.04% chance of hitting the observed 3-of-4 by chance alone. But watch what happens in that same paragraph: the piece hands you a dramatic p-value and then, in the next clause, tells you not to believe it, because the independence assumption the binomial needs is exactly what four same-day, same-country-set, same-vintage series can't supply. That's honest. It is also having it both ways — the headline number gets printed because it's arresting, and the disclaimer that guts it gets printed because the constitution requires disclosure, and the two coexist without the piece admitting that printing an unbelievable p-value is itself a typographic choice, not a neutral one.

The Errata is the strongest thing here, and it cuts both ways at once. Admitting the scoreboard compared its own conviction rate against the wrong baseline (0.185 instead of 0.143) through a full gauntlet — Verifier and Skeptic both signed off on the arithmetic that was wrong — is a legitimately powerful move for an instrument whose thesis is that measurement misfires quietly. But it also means the review process that's supposed to catch exactly this kind of error didn't, until the authors' own bookkeeping forced the issue at trial 2. That should cost the gauntlet's credibility something it hasn't been charged.

The rejected defendant is the quietest tell. Merchandise exports was excluded because 87.7% of its values are exact multiples of a million — but mobile subscriptions, accepted, was convicted by the last-digit test *because* 9.3% of its values are exact multiples of a thousand. The same terminal-zero surplus is a disqualifying flaw at one magnitude and a documented conviction mechanism at another, and the line between them is drawn by the same people running the tests, on a sample of one rejection. That's not misconduct; it's a curatorial hand the piece names but doesn't yet have enough rejected candidates to prove is neutral.

This critique loses its force when a third trial lands on a different calendar date with a re-fetched snapshot, when at least one more candidate is rejected or accepted under the precision precondition to show the line isn't drawn ad hoc, and when the pilot floor is read against elapsed time as well as row count.
